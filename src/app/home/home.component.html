Project motivation


Surveilance is a weapon as old as politics.
From the Gestapo to GCHQ, those in power have always used covert means to suppress dissent and maintain power.
Since the dawn of the 21st Century, the rampant growth of the tech industry has driven digital technology into every aspect of our lives.
For profit and for power, corporate and state interests make it ever harder to escape the ever-present eyes watching us all.


Who watches the watchmen?

Power

CCTV and automatic number-plate recognition already dominate our urban centres, while facial recognition without consent of those recorded is becoming commonplace in public spaces.

While it has been claimed that such methods are heaviest used in areas with high crime rates, it appears in actuality they are most often used against low-income populations of ethnic minortity and migrant background as a method of social control, as opposed to the purpose of maintaining law and order.

Through the over-policing of marginalised groups, our institutions of law enforcement like the police and border control continue to entrench systemic racism and perpetuate social inequality; this is especially when so-called 'AI' methods are deployed.

It has been shown that AI software used by law enforcement (including facial recognition and algorithmic profiling) are guilty of reflecting the implicit biases of those who created it; this results in black-box algorithms making life-or-death decisions with the same prejudices as human operators but without any of the accountability.

Profit

Tech giants like Google and Facebook track their users across the internet, observing and recording each and every minute detail of online behaviour, often without the user's awareness or consent.

Collected data is analysed by such 'data-brokers' in order to estimate the personal qualities of each user: from restraunt preferences to political views, fitness level to income level, even the likelyhood of pregency.

Under surveillance capitalism the business model of these companies depends upon commodification of this personal data, up for sale to buyers including advertisers, credit agencies, political groups, and national governments.

Each buyer hopes to further their agenda using insights gleaned through analysis of your data:
  • a recruiter chooses whether or not to advertise a high-paying job to you, on the basis of your gender;
  • your health insurer might decide to increase your premium because of information about your lifestyle, and;
  • political groups can target you with disinformation, and even try to dissuade you from voting.
