Surveilance is a weapon as old as politics.

From the Gestapo to GCHQ, those in power have always used covert observation to suppress dissent and maintain power.

Since the dawn of the 21st Century, thanks to the rampant growth of the tech industry, digital technology has crept into every aspect of our lives.

For profit and for power, the corporate and state interests behind this technology have made it increasingly difficult to escape the ever-present eyes watching over us all.

Power

CCTV and number-plate recognition already dominate our urban centres, while facial recognition without consent of those recorded is now used in public spaces.

These methods of social control are used heavily in areas with ethnic minority and migrant populations, under the guise of maintaining law and order.

The over-policing of marginalised groups entrenches institutional racism and increases social inequality, especially when so-called AI methods facial recognition and algorithmic profiling are deployed.

It has been shown that AI tends to reflect the biases implicit in it's creators, expressed via the selection of training data, resulting in black-box algorithms making life-or-death decisions with the same prejudices as human operators but without any of the accountability.

In cities like London with dense CCTV coverage and reliance on cashless transactions (especially on public transport), the state is free to scrutinise all aspects of everyday life with a complete lack of oversight.

Profit

Tech giants including Google and Facebook track their users across the internet, observing and recording each minute detail of online behaviour.

The business model of companies in the surveillance capitalism sector depends upon commodification of this personal data, for sale to the highest bidder; this may be

  • commercial entities using tracking advertising;
  • financial firms making decisions on credit-worthiness, and;
  • political groups directing targeted campaigns of (mis)information.

Governments commonly demand and receive access to collected personal data, adding another layer of continuous monitoring to our lives in the name of national security.

Let's turn the tables.
